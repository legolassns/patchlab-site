Custom Iron-On Patches

"Iron-on" doesn't refer to a technique, but to the back of the patch: a heat-activated layer that allows hot application onto the garment. It's available across several techniques, subject to an assessment of the project and the fabric.

The concept

What iron-on means

An iron-on patch is a patch — embroidered, Woven HD or sublimated — with a layer of heat-activated adhesive on the back. It's applied using heat, with a heat press or an iron: the heat activates the adhesive and fixes the patch to the fabric.

The front of the patch remains whatever technique was chosen. Iron-on only concerns the back and the application method: it's an alternative to sewing and hook-and-loop, not a separate technology.

How well the hot application holds depends on the garment, the applicable temperature and the conditions of use. That's why iron-on backing is always offered subject to a technical review of the project.

Assessment

When it's useful and when it needs caution

Hot application is convenient, but it isn't the right answer for every project. These criteria help you find your way.

Useful when

You need a quick application without sewing; the garment is made of a heat-compatible fabric; patches need to be applied independently across multiple garments; the project doesn't involve frequently removing the patch.

Requires caution when

The garment is made of a technical, heat-sensitive or treated fabric (waterproofed, coated); washing is frequent or intensive; the patch is subject to constant stress. In these cases, we assess together whether iron-on is suitable or whether another solution is preferable.

Iron-on, hook-and-loop or sewing?

Iron-on fixes the patch securely but not interchangeably. Hook-and-loop lets you remove and swap the patch. Sewing is the most durable solution for intensive use. These are three different systems, not three versions of the same thing.

Safety stitching

On some projects, it can make sense to add perimeter stitching after hot application, to increase stability over time. It's not a rule: it's an option we assess case by case.

Fabrics

Fabrics and compatibility

Hot application isn't compatible with every fabric. A preliminary check is part of our work.

Generally suitable fabrics

Cotton and natural fabrics that are heat-stable are generally the best candidates for iron-on application. Confirmation always comes from assessing the specific garment.

Fabrics to check

Synthetics, stretch fabrics, coated or treated materials and garments with technical membranes require a specific assessment: the heat needed for adhesion may not be compatible with the material.

What we need to know

Garment type, fabric composition (if known) and intended conditions of use: with this information we can tell you whether iron-on is a sensible choice for your project.

The process

How we assess an iron-on project

Before confirming iron-on backing, we follow these steps.

  1. 01

    Project analysis

    We assess the chosen front technique (embroidered, Woven HD or sublimated), the dimensions and the patch's intended use.

  2. 02

    Fabric check

    We check the garment's compatibility with hot application. If the fabric isn't suitable, we suggest an alternative: sewing or hook-and-loop.

  3. 03

    Solution confirmation

    We confirm the most suitable application system and, where it makes sense, consider adding safety stitching.

  4. 04

    Production and application guidance

    We produce the patches with the agreed backing and provide guidance for correct application.

Frequently asked questions

Answers to the most common questions

The questions we hear most often about iron-on backing. If yours isn't here, get in touch directly.

  • Can an iron-on patch be applied to any fabric?

    No. How well it holds depends on the fabric, the applicable temperature and the conditions of use. Technical, heat-sensitive or treated fabrics require a specific assessment; in some cases sewing or hook-and-loop are more suitable solutions.

  • Is hot application permanent?

    Hot application is designed as a stable fixing, but its durability depends on the garment and its use. For projects subject to frequent washing or constant stress, safety stitching can make sense: we assess it case by case, it isn't a universal rule.

  • Which techniques can have iron-on backing?

    Embroidered, Woven HD and sublimated patches, subject to a technical review of the project. At the moment we don't offer it on PVC patches.

  • Do iron-on patches withstand washing?

    Washing performance depends on the fabric, the quality of the application and washing conditions. Before confirming use with frequent or industrial washing, we assess the project: we don't promise results we can't verify.

  • Iron-on or hook-and-loop — which is better?

    It depends on the use. Iron-on fixes the patch securely to the garment; hook-and-loop makes it removable and interchangeable. If the patch needs to change often, hook-and-loop is the natural choice; if it needs to stay in place, iron-on or sewing.
