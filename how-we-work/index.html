Principles

The PatchLab method

There's no single right answer for every logo, every quantity and every use. What does exist is a process of assessment.

01
Principle

We don't start from the price

Before talking about costs, we assess the project: artwork, the right technique, quantity, final use. The price is the conclusion of an assessment, not the starting point.

02
Principle

The technique must be chosen based on the actual design

There's no single right technique for every logo: the choice depends on size, graphic details, use and final format. Same quantity, different logo — different choice.

03
Principle

An archived project is worth years

Files, the approved sample, colours and notes from the first order allow precise reorders even years later — without starting from scratch every time.

Process

Eight steps, one project

From the first enquiry to delivery — and into the archive for future reorders.

01
Enquiry

We receive your request

Every project starts with the basic information: available artwork, approximate quantity, desired dimensions, intended use and timing. The more details we get upfront, the sooner the proposal arrives.

02
Analysis

We analyse artwork, quantity and use

The artwork file is assessed relative to the final size: text legibility, number of colours, design complexity, the presence of shading or thin lines. We don't confirm feasibility without having seen the actual file.

03
Technique

We assess the most suitable technique

Embroidered, woven, PVC or hook-and-loop: the choice is never automatic. It depends on the logo, the use, the required backing and aesthetic expectations. In many cases, the best proposal isn't the one the customer originally had in mind.

04
Proposal

We prepare the proposal and timeline

Once the technique is defined, we prepare the quote with prices, sampling times and production lead times. If the artwork needs adjustments for the technique, we flag them before starting.

05
Machine file

We create or check the machine file

Digitising — translating the design into machine instructions — is the step that determines the final quality. A well-made machine file produces a good patch. A badly made one produces a poor patch at any price.

06
Sample

We produce the sample

Before starting batch production, we make the physical sample. The customer approves it — or requests changes — before a single additional piece is made. There are no exceptions to this step.

07
Production

We produce and inspect

Batch production takes place after the sample is approved. Every batch is checked before delivery: colours, dimensions, border, backing. We don't deliver defective pieces.

08
Archive

We archive the project

The machine file, approved sample, technical specifications and production notes are kept on file. A reorder a year later picks up exactly where we left off — without redoing the digitising or the sample, if the specifications haven't changed.

Approach

Why we assess before we produce

A patch isn't always embroidered. It isn't always woven. It isn't always PVC. Choosing the wrong technique produces the wrong result — even with the best technical execution.

A logo with fine, small text, embroidered too densely, produces illegible letters. The same logo in woven, or with a font adjustment before digitising, produces a professional result.

Someone who knows the process spots the problem before it becomes a faulty piece.

Before even asking you what you want, we need to understand what works for that design, at that size, for that use.

Continuity

The first order builds knowledge

Reordering should be the simplest step — not the most complicated.

Archive

How the archive works

After every order, we archive the final machine file, a copy of the approved sample, the complete technical specifications and production notes. This data remains available for future reorders.

Reorder

What changes on a reorder

If the specifications don't change — same artwork, same dimensions, same backing — the reorder starts from the file already validated. There's no need to pay for digitising again or remake the sample. The process is significantly faster and less costly than the first order.

Before we start

What we need to prepare a quote

You don't need to have everything on day one. But the more information arrives with the request, the sooner the proposal follows.

Useful information

  • Logo or artwork in vector format (AI, EPS, PDF) or high resolution (PNG, JPG at 300 dpi)
  • Approximate quantity — even a rough range helps shape the proposal
  • Desired dimensions in millimetres, or a physical reference (comparison with an object)
  • Intended use — what type of garment or surface the patch will be applied to
  • Approximate date of use — needed to assess production lead times
  • Application type — sew-on, iron-on, hook-and-loop, or to be defined
  • Any previous patch — if a version has already been produced in the past, it's useful to see it

If you don't have all these details yet, send us what you have. We'll start assessing and guide you through gathering the rest.
